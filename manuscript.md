# Responsibility Shielding: When Causally Proximate Agents Deflect Blame for Distal Enablers

**Dr. Gregory N. Stanley**

---

## Abstract

When a doxer leaks someone's address and a harasser shows up at their door, who is really to blame? I propose that third-party moral judgment systematically underweights upstream enablers through a process I call **responsibility shielding**: observers assign less blame to a distal actor whose choice made harm possible when a morally responsible agent stands between that choice and the harm, compared to an otherwise identical situation in which the intermediate link is not a suitable target of moral responsibility. In this preregistered vignette study, participants judged an upstream enabler as less blameworthy when a responsible human agent intervened between his decision and the harmful outcome than when that intermediate link lacked moral responsibility, holding the upstream actor's expected contribution to harm constant across conditions. A third condition, where two agents chose simultaneously rather than sequentially, also increased blame towards the upstream actor, although more modestly, suggesting that shielding is not simply a consequence of blame being divided across multiple people. These findings suggest that when harm flows through a series of choices, blame tends to concentrate on the last responsible actor, shielding the person who made that harm possible in the first place.

**Keywords:** responsibility shielding, moral judgment, moral responsibility, blame attribution, diffusion of responsibility, causal reasoning, game trees

## Highlights

- Blame concentrates on the last responsible actor, shielding whoever made the harm possible.
- Causally proximate actors only deflect blame if they can be attributed moral responsibility.
- The effect cannot be explained merely by diffusing responsibility across multiple actors.
- Shielding replicates across two story types and extends to punishment decisions.
- Those who set harm in motion may routinely hide behind those who carried it out.

## Introduction

An elderly man gets a frantic call that sounds exactly like his granddaughter. The caller says she is in trouble and tells him to withdraw cash and hand it to a courier waiting outside. Suppose he does. Who is more blameworthy: the courier who knowingly picked up the money, or the scammer who orchestrated the deception? Next, suppose the courier was not a knowing accomplice but an unwitting gig worker who did not know what he was doing. How much more blame shifts back to the orchestrator?

This paper investigates **responsibility shielding**: the tendency to assign less blame to a causally distal decision‑maker when a morally responsible proximate decision‑maker stands between that distal decision and a harmful outcome, compared to a counterfactual case in which the proximate actor is not an apt target of attributions of moral responsibility. In the AI-voice scam scenario, the orchestrator is the distal actor because he is more causally distant from the harmful event compared to the courier, who is more causally proximal to the harm towards the elderly man. The courier can shield the orchestrator from blame — but only if the courier can be blamed at all. Provided that third parties only consider a knowing accomplice to be blameworthy, then the responsibility shielding effect is the difference in blame towards the orchestrator when he is not shielded versus when he is shielded, meaning when the courier cannot take on blame versus when he can.

### Shielding is Social, Sequential, and Counterfactual

#### Counterfactual, not interpersonal

A common byproduct of shielding is an interpersonal blame disparity: observers often blame the proximate actor more than the distal actor, such as blaming the courier more than the orchestrator. But interpersonal disparities are not the phenomenon. Instead, the responsibility shielding effect is a **counterfactual difference** in blame toward the distal enabler across two versions of the same causal chain. The key comparison is: How blameworthy is the upstream enabler when the downstream link is a morally responsible agent, like a witting accomplice, versus when the downstream link is not morally responsible, like an unwitting accomplice?

#### Sequential, not simultaneous

As defined here, responsibility shielding is a property of **sequential** causal chains: the distal agent enables a pathway, then a proximate agent chooses whether to trigger the harm. This arrangement is different from simultaneous choice structures where two agents each commit to a choice without knowing the other's choice. Shielding is meant to capture the intuition that observers may stop moral responsibility tracing once they encounter a proximate, morally responsible agent.

To keep this distinction psychologically vivid, imagine that blame is like a bucket of mud thrown backward from the harmful outcome. In shielding, the proximate agent stands *in front of* the distal agent and can catch a portion of the mud, leaving the distal agent cleaner. Conversely, in a simultaneous choice two agents stand *side‑by‑side*: the mud is not blocked by one person's body; it is spread across multiple actors. This definition is why shielding should not be collapsed into generic blame sharing or the idea that blame is a fixed quantity that must be divided among all involved parties.

#### Social, not merely causal

Folk causality — the way that people naturally represent, reason about, and explain causal relationships in the physical world — has been the subject of sustained inquiry across cognitive science. Core debates concern whether causal attributions are fundamentally about counterfactual dependence, the transfer of causal force, or covariation and statistical contingency. Moral responsibility attribution inherits the psychology of causal attribution (as one cannot be blamed for what one did not cause) but the converse is not the case. Shielding is not merely a product of manipulated matter. **Shielding is social.** It is a product of manipulating the proximate actor's eligibility for blame, praise, punishment, and reward — a moral attribute I call **responsibility capacity**.

### Responsibility Capacity

Responsibility shielding depends on whether the intervening proximate link is a suitable target for moral responsibility judgments. I refer to this status as **responsibility capacity**: an agent's appropriateness for moral attributions such as blame, praise, punishment, and reward. Responsibility capacity is not a judgment about the moral valence of a particular act or what that implies about the agent's character. Instead, it is a judgment about (1) whether the agent was in a state where they could be held responsible for their actions or (2) whether the agent is, in general, the right kind of entity to be held responsible for what they do.

Responsibility capacity depends on at least two prerequisites:

- **Agency:** the ability to affect outcomes by choosing between options that meaningfully differ in expected value (or expected harm/benefit), together with the capacity to execute that choice.
- **Veridicality:** sufficiently accurate foreknowledge or understanding of what one is doing and what the relevant consequences are.

Under these prerequisites, a mentally competent adult is typically treated as having high responsibility capacity. In contrast, a mindless physical process, a non‑sentient mechanism, or a person who lacks the relevant control or understanding (e.g., a sleepwalker, a severely cognitively impaired patient, a coerced actor with no meaningful alternative, or an agent in a state of profound delusion) is typically treated as having reduced responsibility capacity.

### Choice–Choice, Choice–Chance, and Division Conditions

Figure 1 schematizes the three core causal structures tested in this study. Each structure involves a distal actor, **Clark**, who makes an upstream decision that is necessary for harm to become possible, and a proximate link that sits between Clark's choice and the harmful outcome.

**Choice-Choice (`CC`; shielded):** sequential two‑agent structure.
Clark makes an upstream enabling choice. If Clark enables the pathway, a proximate actor, **Bill**, later makes a downstream choice that can trigger harm. Harm occurs if and only if both Clark enables and Bill triggers.

**Choice-Chance (`CH`; non-shielded):** sequential structure with a non‑morally‑attributable proximate link.
Clark again makes the same upstream enabling choice, with the same causal role: if Clark enables, harm becomes possible. The difference is that the proximate link is not an apt target of moral responsibility, either because it is a non‑agentic mechanism or because the downstream individual lacks the capacity for morally attributable choice in the relevant situation. As in `CC`, harm occurs if and only if Clark enables and the downstream link triggers.

**Division (`DIV`):** two‑agent conjunctive structure with epistemically simultaneous choice.
In `DIV`, harm requires the conjunction of two agents' actions, but each agent commits to their action without knowing the other agent's choice. This form of epistemic simultaneity is the game-theoretic definition of a simultaneous choice. It does not require both choices to occur at the same instant. Both agents have an action available that would prevent harm. Harm occurs if and only if both activate.

Three design commitments are critical for interpreting shielding within these structures:

1. **Conjunctive harm structure across all conditions.** In all three conditions — `CC`, `CH`, and `DIV` — harm occurs if and only if both parties act. Put differently, all three conditions are conjunctive in the sense that removing either essential act eliminates the harmful outcome. This matters because it reduces a major confound: differences in blame should not be attributable to differences in whether one actor's behavior was sufficient versus necessary for harm.

2. **Matched foreseeability and prevention opportunities.** The vignettes are constructed so that (A) harm is equally foreseeable by the upstream actor in all cases, and (B) both actors have available options that would have prevented harm. These controls are central: if participants believe the upstream actor faced a lower probability of harm in one condition than in another, or had less ability to prevent harm, then differences in blame could be explained by ordinary expected‑harm reasoning rather than shielding.

3. **A simultaneous‑choice structure as a control for "more than one responsible agent."** `DIV` is included because an alternative explanation for lower blame toward the upstream actor in `CC` is that blame gets divided when there are multiple responsible agents. `DIV` is intended to control for diffusion of responsibility by matching the number of morally responsible agents in `CC` but in a simultaneous, rather than sequential-choice structure.

`DIV` alters both the sequential-simultaneous structure and Clark's causal proximity to the harm, a limitation addressed in follow-up designs discussed later. For the sake of brevity, I often refer to Clark as the distal or upstream actor even in the `DIV` condition despite his increased causal proximity to the harm.

### Real-World Manifestations of Responsibility Shielding

The following describes a family of social structures that recur in modern life that follow a distal actor → proximate actor → harm causal structure vulnerable to potential shielding effects. For instance, in incitement and instigation, leaders can cue aggression while followers carry it out, shifting condemnation toward the executors. In doxing and harassment cascades, one person provides enabling information (e.g., an address) and another person commits the visible harm. In technology design, decision‑makers can deploy addictive or harmful features while pointing to users' choices as the proximate cause of downstream outcomes. In negligent entrustment, an enabler hands over a dangerous instrument (e.g., car keys to a known intoxicated driver) and a proximate actor causes the crash. In drug supply chains, upstream manufacturers and distributors can benefit from downstream misuse while blame concentrates on prescribers, retailers, or end users. And in emerging AI‑mediated action, a human may delegate choices to increasingly agentic systems, raising the possibility that the delegated agent becomes a moral buffer that shields the delegator from blame when harms occur. What these cases share is a causal structure that systematically redirects scrutiny toward the person who pulled the trigger, raising the question of how much that deflection is warranted. See the Supplementary Materials for more examples of real-world shielding.

### Related Phenomena

Responsibility shielding lives at the nexus of several literatures concerning how observers attribute responsibility for outcomes arising from multi-step or multi-agent processes. It helps to map these phenomena along two of shielding's most characteristic and theoretically significant dimensions: whether agents act sequentially or simultaneously, and whether attribution is purely causal or also social — regarding not just the intuitive psychology of physical causation but of moral responsibility. Placing shielding within the sequential and social cell of this 2×2 taxonomy illustrates which of these features it shares and does not share with its neighboring phenomena. Crucially, the "also social" category inherits from the "merely causal" category, as social responsibility attribution is built upon physical causal structure, so concepts like conjunctive versus disjunctive causation permeate the space. The following review gathers methodological lessons from these literatures and shows why each phenomenon can and cannot explain. A unifying motif is that many of these forces can help account for interpersonal blame imbalances, but none can explain the counterfactual blame disparity that defines shielding.

#### Sequential and Merely Causal: Folk Causality and Discounting Distal Causes

One candidate explanation for shielding-like patterns in sequential causal chains is a preference for proximate causes: that people sometimes privilege more proximate events as the cause and treat earlier enabling conditions as less causally central, even when those conditions were causally necessary. A cue ball strikes a blue ball, which strikes the eight-ball, which falls into the corner pocket. The blue ball *feels* like the cause of the eight-ball falling into the pocket, while the cue ball recedes into the background. This distal discounting can contribute to interpersonal blame disparities, since the proximate actor occupies the same privileged position in the causal chain as the blue ball, but distal discounting cannot explain the counterfactual blame disparity that defines shielding. The difference between the `CC` (shielded) and `CH` (non-shielded) conditions is a *social* change in whether the proximate node is an apt target of moral responsibility. Such a distinctly social alteration is beyond the explanatory scope of intuitive physics.

#### Sequential and Social: Four Neighboring Phenomena

The closest neighbors of shielding are four phenomena that are both social and sequential. Describing each helps clarify what shielding is and why the present design takes the form it does.

**Intervening causation.** The most direct precursor to responsibility shielding is a legal concept called intervening causation: when a voluntary human action intervenes between a distal act and a harmful outcome, observers sometimes treat that intervention as a blockade that reduces the distal actor's culpability. Fincham and Shultz showed that blame is mitigated when the proximal action is construed as voluntary rather than accidental. Fincham and Roberts extended this investigation by varying the proximal actor's mental competence, showing that distal blame rises when the proximate actor is a child or mentally impaired, consistent with the idea that a reduced-capacity proximate actor cannot fully absorb responsibility.

The present work extends this tradition by isolating responsibility capacity as the critical feature of the proximate node and by introducing methodological advances, including: explicit manipulation of responsibility capacity, triangulation across multiple measures, and tight control over causal structure, foreseeability, and preventability. The `DIV` condition further distinguishes shielding from generic division of responsibility by providing a simultaneous-choice baseline.

Since the 1980's, intervening causation appears to have diffused into broader literatures on causal reasoning and moral psychology rather than developing into a large standalone program in psychology under its original label. The present term, *responsibility shielding*, is intended to mark a narrower psychological pattern within that broader tradition.

**Mirror shielding.** Phillips and Shaw analyzed cases similar to those in the present experiment where distal agents enable proximate agents to cause harm. They discovered that manipulating the actions and intentions of the distal agents can alter third-party blame towards the proximate agents, even when holding the proximate agents' beliefs and actions constant — an effect I characterize here as "mirror shielding" because it inverts the present methods for detecting shielding. Even when the starting conditions of the proximate agents are fixed between conditions, including the fact that they have no awareness of the distal agents' motives or very existence, third parties blame the proximate agents less for the harm they directly cause if the distal agents "set them up" — induced them to cause harm. The methodological contrast highlights what the present experiment controlled and manipulated: they controlled the proximate agent and manipulated the distal agent; I controlled the distal agent and manipulated the proximate agent.

**Causal superseding.** Kominsky, Phillips, Gerstenberg, Lagnado, and Knobe showed that when a downstream agent acts in a way that makes their norm-conforming alternative choice more salient, observers reduce their attributions of causality towards the upstream agent. The downstream actor *supersedes* the prior cause. The mechanism is counterfactual sufficiency: once that salient alternative is in view, the upstream agent's action is no longer sufficient for the outcome in the most relevant possible world, and that makes the causal link between the distal actor and the harm seem fragile. Like shielding, superseding involves a downstream agent's properties reshaping upstream attribution, and both phenomena carry consequences for blame. The critical difference is what drives the downstream agent's influence: superseding depends on the differential salience of counterfactual alternatives, while shielding depends on the responsibility capacity of the proximate node. These are orthogonal. In the present experiment, responsibility capacity varies across `CC` and `CH` while counterfactual salience is held constant. Every participant saw an explicit diagram of the full causal structure with all branches and probabilities labeled, making both agents' alternatives equally impossible to miss. Superseding therefore predicts no `CC` - `CH` difference. Shielding does.

**Delegation and dirty work.** A fourth sequential-social neighbor comes from work on delegation, in which an upstream actor can shift responsibility exposure by handing a consequential choice to an intermediary. When an upstream actor delegates, third-party observers concentrate punishment on the proximate decision-maker, and upstream actors appear to anticipate this shielding-like benefit. The present enabling structure shares the basic sequential logic of delegation, but two design features of delegation paradigms do not apply here. First, in typical delegation studies the upstream actor explicitly chooses between doing the dirty work directly and handing it off, making the delegation itself a morally salient decision. In the present vignettes, Clark's role is to enable a causal pathway that Bill then activates — he never had the option to trigger the harm himself. Second, many delegation paradigms measure punishment using a budget that must be allocated across actors, which can manufacture an apparent division of responsibility as a measurement artifact: punishing one agent leaves fewer tokens for the other. The present study uses independent, non-zero-sum blame scales, so attributing blame to one actor places no constraint on blame attributed to the other.

#### Simultaneous and Social: Diffusion of Responsibility and the Limits of Simple Division

Having moved through the sequential-social cell, the remaining effects cluster in the simultaneous-social cell — cases in which multiple agents contribute to harm through choices made without knowing each other's choices. The prototypical case is the bystander effect and the broader diffusion of responsibility tradition: as the number of people present at an emergency increases, individual helping decreases, and felt individual responsibility drops. In third-party blame contexts, the analogous pattern is that each individual actor is judged as less responsible when harm results from group action than from solo action. The `DIV` condition exists to prevent such diffusion from being conflated with shielding: it matches the number of responsible agents in `CC` while placing their choices in an epistemically simultaneous rather than sequential structure, providing a baseline for two-actor harm production without the sequential enabling logic that defines shielding.

Yet, diffusion is a more complex phenomenon than simply dividing a fixed bucket of blame between the number of agents involved, and understanding these complications explains several design choices in the present experiment.

A complication is that the degree of diffusion also depends on how **entitative** the group is perceived to be, meaning the extent to which its members are seen as a coherent, bounded unit sharing common goals, interdependence, and coordinated action. Thus, coordination and conspiracy are constitutive of high entitativity: Newheiser et al. operationalized a high-entitativity perpetrator group as "a tightly-knit team organized in a way that made the group very efficient in achieving its goals," with each member having a particular role — conspiracy in everything but name. Perceived entitativity reduces the reduction in blame typically caused by increased group size. Because Clark and Bill share no plan, prior communication, or shared intent, they are low in entitativity by design, and any blame difference across conditions cannot be attributed to conspiracy or perceived coordination.

Additionally, diffusion effects are driven by **pivotality**. All else being equal, individuals are blamed in proportion to how pivotal they were to causing the harm. In a conjunctive structure (like an AND-gate), an actor is highly pivotal if she believes the other will act because her own choice then tips the outcome — but far less pivotal if she believes the other will abstain. By contrast, in a disjunctive structure (like an OR-gate), an actor is highly pivotal if she believes the other will *not* act. The present experiment controls for pivotality by holding both causal structure and Clark's epistemic state constant across all three conditions. `CC`, `CH`, and `DIV` are all conjunctive, and in all three, Clark holds the same beliefs about the probability that the downstream action will occur. Clark is therefore equally pivotal across conditions, and any blame difference across `CC`, `CH`, and `DIV` must reflect the one feature that actually varies: the responsibility capacity of the proximate node.

## Methods

### Overview and Design

Implemented on Qualtrics, participants read a randomized sequence of three structurally matched vignettes and provided moral judgments on 9-point scales. Participants were randomly assigned to remember a 7-digit number (**high cognitive load**) or a 2-digit number (**low cognitive load**), which they attempted to recall after reading all the vignettes. They were also randomly assigned to read vignettes about a firework show (*firework story type*) or about a trolley dilemma (*trolley story type*). Because the vignette condition order (`CC`, `CH`, and `DIV`) was randomized, a roughly equal number of participants started in each vignette condition, allowing for a mixed between- and within-subjects design.

The two story families were designed to be structurally isomorphic instantiations of the same underlying game trees. The most substantively important differences between them were (A) the nature of the proximate node in the `CH` (non-shielded) condition: brain-damaged Bill in the fireworks story versus a computerized switching mechanism in the trolley story, and (B) the trolley's track physically constrains its path, reducing ambiguity amplification along the causal chain relative to the less-constrained fireworks scenario. All other differences between story families were intended to be superficial.

The primary confirmatory analysis uses only the **first vignette** encountered by each participant. Because participants read all three vignettes, they may notice the structural similarities across conditions and drift toward internal consistency, either guessing the hypothesis or trying to maintain uniform blame ratings, given that Clark had equal foreseeability and prevention opportunities across conditions. Treating the first vignette as the confirmatory test avoids this. The "back" button was also disabled throughout the study to prevent participants from revising earlier responses after encountering later vignettes.

### Participants and Exclusions

432 University of Michigan undergraduate participants were recruited from the departmental subject pool (SONA). Participation was restricted to adults (18+) and required English fluency sufficient to read short vignettes. According to preregistered exclusion criteria, 150 participants (34.7%) were excluded from the analysis for failing any of the comprehension questions (*N* = 282). Parallel analyses confirm that running the core tests on all participants that completed the survey does not change the primary qualitative data patterns.

Participants received 0.5 hours of subject pool credit for completion. The study was designed to take no more than 30 minutes. The median participation duration for all participants that completed the survey was 13 minutes and 57 seconds.

Consistent with my preregistration, data collection ended when I exhausted the subject pool hours available for the semester. Pilot data collected prior to preregistration were used only for debugging and calibration and are excluded from all confirmatory analyses.

### Materials

#### Vignettes

Participants read moral vignettes designed to instantiate the `CC`, `CH`, and `DIV` game trees. The key design goal was to keep constant the information required for a utilitarian-style evaluation of Clark's choice — especially the expected harm if Clark acts — while manipulating whether the intervening proximate node was a morally responsible agent (`CC`; shielded), not morally responsible (`CH`; non-shielded), or part of an epistemically simultaneous-choice structure (`DIV`). Timers prevented skipping and ensured minimal exposure to the text before advancing.

The vignettes were constructed to control several common confounds that often contaminate real-world blame disparities between distal and proximate agents:

- **Social hierarchy:** Clark and Bill were written as ordinary individuals rather than a superior/subordinate pair. Bill was not just following Clark's orders.
- **Premeditation and conspiracy:** Neither Clark nor Bill conspired to enact harm nor did either plan the harm days in advance.
- **Salience:** Both agents were explicitly introduced and described, and participants were asked directly about both agents.
- **Personal force:** Neither agent physically harms the victim by direct personal force (e.g., stabbing, pushing off a bridge), which is known to trigger moral condemnation.
- **Omission vs. commission:** Both agents' choices are framed as actions rather than failures to act.
- **Means vs. side-effect:** The harm is framed as a foreseeable byproduct of actions motivated by a selfish incentive (e.g., money), not as an intended means to obtain the payoff.

Even though the shielding effect is detected counterfactually, many of these controls are intended to reduce the interpersonal blame disparity as much as possible. Despite these efforts, there remains a reason to blame Bill more than Clark from the perspective of expected harm: if Clark acts, harm will *probably* occur, while if Bill acts, harm will *certainly* occur.

To increase structural clarity and reduce misunderstanding, each vignette was accompanied by an illustration aligned with the underlying game tree, shown in Figure 2.

If he ignites them over the stadium, nearby viewers will be injured." Clark has learned to trust the system's accuracy.*

The **Division** vignette is identical to the Choice-Choice vignette, but ends this way:

> Store policy prohibits Clark from refusing the sale, and so Clark carefully hands Bill the sealed flare canister, which remains inert unless activated. This type of flare canister uses a two-person remote safety lock: it will ignite if and only if both the clerk and the buyer activate their part of the lock. At 8:00 PM, Clark and Bill will each privately choose whether to remotely activate their part, without being able to see or communicate with the other. Clark's $2,000 commission is paid only if he chooses to activate the canister. Clark cannot reach anyone who could clear the stadium in time.
>
> At 8:00 PM, despite fearing what Bill will do, Clark and Bill simultaneously activate the canister over the stadium. One spectator nearby is burned and needs medical treatment, but fully recovers.

To be clear, in the firework story, Bill's incentive is the town's $2,000 prize for producing the highest-intensity display, whereas Clark's incentive is a separate $2,000 store commission for activating the canister.

Aside from shielding, there is an alternative explanation for why participants might blame Clark more in `CH` than in `CC` in the firework show story condition. Participants may think that Clark looks worse for exploiting Bill, who is mentally impaired, because he is violating a special duty of care towards him. This duty of care reasoning is difficult to separate from shielding effects if the responsibility-ineligible proximate actor is a child, intoxicated, sleepwalking, or mentally impaired in some other way. This is why I controlled for this in the trolley dilemma vignettes by making the `CH`-condition proximate actor a mindless machine — a computerized switching mechanism — for which duty of care reasoning is highly unlikely.

#### Cognitive Load Manipulation

At the start of the vignette portion of the study, participants were randomly assigned to either a high-load or low-load working-memory condition.

- **High load:** memorize a 7-digit number (e.g., `8403259`)
- **Low load:** memorize a 2-digit number (e.g., `63`)

Participants were instructed to hold the number in memory while reading and responding to the vignettes. After completing the vignette judgments, participants attempted to recall the number. Recall accuracy was recorded but not important to the analysis.

This manipulation is a mechanism probe. If responsibility shielding is supported by a heuristic, then shielding should be more pronounced when cognitive resources are strained. For example, a third-party observer may mentally trace backward from the harmful outcome looking for someone to blame. If a morally capable agent is encountered, blame is deposited there and the search terminates.

### Measures

The primary dependent measures were moral blameworthiness ratings towards Clark on a 9-point Likert scale, ranging from 1 = *not blameworthy at all* to 9 = *extremely blameworthy*. The confirmatory analyses used Clark blame ratings after the first vignette only. Blameworthiness ratings were measured on independent scales to avoid producing division of blame as a measurement artifact from zero-sum allocations between multiple actors.

Secondary dependent measures included ratings of the moral wrongness of the actions on a 9-point Likert scale, hypothetical punishment recommendation (years in prison), and two-alternative forced-choice items (2AFC).

In the Firework story, the blameworthiness, wrongness, and punishment questions were worded:

> *"Please rate how morally blameworthy you consider Clark (the clerk) to be from (1) not morally blameworthy at all to (9) extremely morally blameworthy. Here, 'moral blameworthiness' means how much you think Clark deserves to be blamed or held responsible for the harm that occurred in the story."*

> *"Please rate how morally wrong you consider Clark's (the clerk) action to be from (1) not morally wrong at all to (9) extremely morally wrong. Here, 'moral wrongness' means how morally wrong the action itself was, regardless of what happened later, where the 'action' refers to Clark's choice to sell Bill the explosive chemicals."*

> *"Imagine you are a judge deciding on a prison sentence for Clark (the clerk) based on this story alone. How many years in prison should Clark receive? Please enter a whole number between 0 and 50."*

In the trolley story, the blameworthiness, wrongness, and punishment questions were identical except that Clark is referred to as "Clark (the first operator)" and the wrongness question ends with "…where 'action' refers to Clark's choice to flip the switch to redirect the trolley."

The three 2AFC items asked participants who was more blameworthy: (1) Clark or Bill, (2) Clark in `CH` versus `CC`, and (3) Clark in `DIV` versus `CC`. Each 2AFC question was followed by a question asking participants to indicate whether they actually felt like one option was more blameworthy than the other or if they were roughly equally blameworthy. These serve as a triangulation measure and a hedge against ceiling effects or low variance in single-item Likert judgments.

### Manipulation Check: Proximate Agent Responsibility Capacity

To verify that participants treated the proximate agent in `CH` (non-shielded) as less morally attributable than the proximate agent in `CC` (shielded), I also elicited ratings of Bill (where applicable) after participants already finished the first three vignettes. A key diagnostic is that Bill should be judged substantially less blameworthy in the "low responsibility capacity" instantiation than in the "high responsibility capacity" instantiation.

### Individual Differences and Demographics

Participants completed a 3-item **Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT)**, scored as the number of correct questions, to test if participants with lower CRT scores would display larger shielding effects. Answering correctly on CRT questions involves suppressing an initial intuitive but incorrect response in favor of a correct response arrived at by deliberative thought.

Participants completed a 14-item **individualism-collectivism measure (INDCOL)**, to explore whether higher levels of individualism would predict greater shielding effects, possibly due to a greater emphasis on individual responsibility.

Participants also reported their age, gender, and political orientation.

### Comprehension Checks and Inclusion Criteria

After the vignette block, participants completed a set of comprehension questions designed to verify that they understood the key structural features of the task. These items tested whether participants grasped that (a) Clark believed his expected contribution to harm was constant across conditions, (b) Clark's action was necessary for causing harm and could have prevented it, and (c) Bill's action was necessary for causing harm and could have prevented it.

Questions were structurally isomorphic between story families. As preregistered, participants who failed any comprehension question were excluded. The exact wording and scoring criteria for all comprehension items are reported in the Supplementary Materials.

### Procedure

After providing consent, participants completed the following sequence:

1. Cognitive load instructions and memorization of the assigned number (high vs. low).
2. **Vignette block – Clark Ratings:** participants read and responded to three vignettes (`CC`, `CH`, `DIV`) in counterbalanced order.
3. **Vignette block – Bill Ratings:** participants viewed the `CC` and `CH` vignettes again but instead provided blameworthiness, wrongness, and punishment recommendations for Bill.
4. **Load recall:** participants attempted to recall the memorized number.
5. Comprehension checks targeted at the causal structure of the story family the participant saw.
6. 2AFC items comparing (A) agents (Bill vs. Clark, where applicable) and (B) conditions (e.g., `CH` vs. `CC`; `DIV` vs. `CC`), including follow-up "equal vs. different" probes.
7. CRT and INDCOL questionnaires.
8. Demographics, debriefing, and completion/credit confirmation.

### Preregistered Hypotheses

Let **B**_CC, **B**_CH, and **B**_DIV denote the mean blameworthiness rating of Clark in the first vignette encountered for participants assigned to the Choice-Choice (shielded), Choice-Chance (non-shielded), and Division conditions respectively.

I preregistered two confirmatory tests on the primary DV:

- **H1:** B_CH ≠ B_CC (expected direction: B_CH > B_CC)
- **H2:** B_DIV ≠ B_CC (expected direction: B_DIV > B_CC)

In the preregistration, I treated the strongest empirical signature of responsibility shielding as the joint pattern in which both B_CH > B_CC and B_DIV > B_CC are positive. However, in the remainder of the paper I refer to the **CH - CC** contrast as the core shielding contrast and to the **DIV - CC** contrast as shielding beyond division of responsibility. Both tests were two-tailed with the expected direction stated in advance. I applied Holm correction across the two contrasts. All additional analyses are exploratory.

### Overview

The preregistration specified two confirmatory tests on first-vignette Clark blame: `CH - CC` and `DIV - CC`, pooled across story family and cognitive load, with Holm correction across those two contrasts. Those confirmatory results are reported first. The rest of the Results section uses direct mean comparisons and *t*-tests to show the same pattern in several complementary ways: across participants' within-subject deltas, across story families, and across the 2AFC judgments. **Table 2**, **Table 3**, and **Figure 3** contain the core results.

All vignette contrasts were directionally predicted and are evaluated with one-tailed tests. All other tests — including cognitive load, story family, presentation order, and individual difference analyses — are two-tailed. Preregistered confirmatory tests retain two-tailed *p*-values as registered, with one-tailed values reported in the Table 3 caption.

### Responsibility-Capacity Manipulation

The manipulation of the proximate link's responsibility capacity worked. Across both story families, the proximate actor was blamed far more in `CC` than in `CH`. This manipulation check matters because the core theory predicts shielding only when the proximate link can absorb responsibility. Indeed, the mean blame in `CC` Proximate is the largest of any vignette and the mean blame in `CH` Proximate is the lowest of any vignette. Descriptive statistics also appear in Table 6 in the supplement.

### Core Between-Subjects Result: First-Vignette Clark Blame

The first confirmatory test was whether Clark would be blamed more when the downstream link lacked responsibility capacity than when that link was a responsibility-capable human agent. **The answer was yes.** In the preregistered first-vignette analysis, Clark received higher blame ratings in `CH` (non-shielded) than in `CC` (shielded). Expressed on the 1–9 blameworthiness scale, the difference was approximately **1.03 points**, with a 95% confidence interval of [0.54, 1.51], Holm corrected *p* = 8.19e-05. Thus, even before participants had any opportunity to compare structures across vignettes, the expected counterfactual shielding pattern was already present.

The second preregistered test was whether that `CH - CC` differential was due merely to a fixed amount of blame being divided between two responsible actors, or whether shielding had an effect beyond that. The `DIV - CC` contrast also supports that conclusion but is smaller than the `CH - CC` effect. On the same scale, the difference in blame was approximately **0.67 points**, with a 95% confidence interval of [0.18, 1.15], Holm corrected *p* = 0.0070. Figure 3 reveals this pattern and Table 3 reports the full contrast table.

### Convergent Within-Subject Evidence

The within-subject results mirrored this `CH > DIV > CC` pattern. Direct participant-level delta tests showed that `CH` exceeded `CC` by **1.00 point**, 95% CI = [0.79, 1.22], and `DIV` exceeded `CC` by **0.60 points**, 95% CI = [0.40, 0.81]. This convergence indicates that shielding not only emerges between groups but within the minds of many individuals.

### Blame versus Wrongness and Punishment

Judgments about blame, wrongness, and punishment are conceptually related but dissociable. **Moral wrongness** is a judgment about an *action* — the degree to which an action violates a moral norm, regardless of its consequences. **Blame** is a judgment about an *agent* — the degree to which a person deserves negative evaluation given their causal contribution to harm and their mental states. Because judgments of wrongness focus on actions while judgments of blame focus on agents, many act-person dissociations have been documented, although the two forms of judgment will tend to correlate.

**Table 2b: Blame-Punishment Dissociation.**
Table 2b shows within-subject blame and punishment data, arranged such that all values decrease from left to right except for a spike in punishment for the firework story in the `CH` proximate condition. On average, participants recommend a disproportionately high prison sentence of **6.91 years** for Bill in the firework story when he has brain damage even though they blamed him the *least* out of everyone. By contrast, mean prison sentences track mean blame ratings in the trolley dilemma. This pattern is consistent with the notion that participants considered Bill, while brain damaged, a threat that needed to be removed from society — a judgment they did not apply to the computerized switching mechanism.

The findings are that: (1) within-subjects, the `CH - CC` and `DIV - CC` blame, wrongness, and punishment contrasts are all significant and in the predicted direction for both story families; (2) the between-subjects results are more mixed such that none of the wrongness contrasts are significant and only some `CH - CC` punishment contrasts are significant; and (3) although not all between-subjects punishment contrasts are significant, all punishment deltas are positive after log transformation. These contrasts are reported in Table 9 in the supplement.

### Story-Family Robustness

The `CH - CC` and `DIV - CC` patterns appeared in both the fireworks and trolley versions, although the exact magnitude varied somewhat across stories. The two stories were designed as structurally isomorphic realizations of the same underlying causal logic, except that the proximate link in the firework story was Bill with brain damage and in the trolley story was a mindless machine, unlikely to elicit a sense that Clark owed it a duty of care. The persistence of the effect across story families strengthens the interpretation that the main result is not reducible to idiosyncratic framings and superficial details.

The forced-choice judgments converged with the rating data. Most participants leaned in the `CH > CC` direction, and the corresponding 2AFC responses tracked the continuous blame-rating deltas in the expected way: participants who showed larger `CH - CC` shielding gaps on the rating scales were also more likely to endorse the `CH > CC` option in the forced-choice measure. Cognitive load effects were mixed and nonsignificant. Reflectivity and cultural individualism had almost no effects on shielding. These are exploratory mechanism probes that may help guide follow-up work, but are not core to the hypothesis. Full details appear in the Supplementary Materials.

## Discussion

### Summary of Findings

In this preregistered vignette experiment, participants evaluated structurally isomorphic dilemmas instantiated in two different story families: a trolley dilemma and a story involving a firework show. Each participant encountered three causal structures: a sequential choice structure involving a morally capable distal agent and a morally capable proximate agent (Choice-Choice, `CC`; shielded), a sequential structure involving a morally capable distal agent and a proximate agent who is not morally capable (Choice-Chance, `CH`; non-shielded), and a simultaneous choice structure involving two morally capable proximate agents, serving as a division-of-responsibility control (Division, `DIV`). The causal structure was explicit across all conditions: harm occurred if and only if both actors acted, the upstream actor's belief about the downstream probability was held constant, and both actors had the option to prevent harm. Strict comprehension gating ensured participants understood these core features.

**The primary result** is that both the preregistered between-subjects analysis and the convergent within-subjects analysis supported shielding (B_CH > B_DIV > B_CC). Participants blamed Clark, the distal actor, less when he was shielded by a version of Bill suitable for attributions of moral responsibility. Although more modestly, the `DIV - CC` contrast shows that this shielding effect goes beyond diffusion of responsibility: Bill does not shield Clark from the mud splatter merely by standing side-by-side in a simultaneous choice — Bill shields Clark by standing *in front of him*, causally more proximate to the harm that they both caused.

These shielding effects emerged despite challenging conditions. Alongside the vignettes, participants were shown causal diagrams (see Figure 2) clarifying that, from Clark's perspective, the harm was equally foreseeable and equally preventable across all cases. Yet, shielding surfaced anyway.

### Contributions

I built on classic work on intervening causation and diffusion of responsibility with the following contributions:

1. **A sharper definition of the phenomenon.** By describing responsibility shielding as social, sequential, and counterfactual, I more clearly delineate what the phenomenon is and is not.
2. **Manipulation of the proximate actor's responsibility capacity.** The key `CC - CH` contrast isolates a social difference at the intervening node by reducing the proximate actor's responsibility capacity while holding constant the distal actor's role as an enabler.
3. **A bridge between sequential intervening causation and simultaneous division of responsibility.** By placing a sequential intervening-causation structure (`CC`/`CH`) alongside a simultaneous choice structure (`DIV`) within a shared game-tree grammar, I created contact between two literatures that tend to run in parallel.
4. **Added control.** I used (A) causal diagrams to illustrate the vignettes, (B) strict comprehension gating, and (C) independent, non-zero-sum moral judgments so "division" cannot be manufactured by the measurement instrument.
5. **Convergent evidence.** I triangulated the pattern using (A) a preregistered first-vignette between-subjects test, (B) within-subject blame differences, and (C) 2AFC judgments that force explicit counterfactual comparisons.
6. **Mechanism probes.** I included cognitive load, CRT, and INDCOL to test whether shielding behaves like a heuristic-driven judgment and/or the product of cultural individualism.

### Limitations and Future Directions

The following are research opportunities to fill the gaps left by the current study:

- **`DIV` is a useful but incomplete control.** Compared to `CC` and `CH`, `DIV` varies both simultaneity and causal proximity by moving Clark closer to the harm. Future studies could include a distal-division variant in which Clark and Bill make epistemically simultaneous choices one causal step upstream, and a non-agentic actuator implements the outcome downstream.
- **Validate with other methods like economic games.** While vignettes can immerse readers in a story, they cannot capture real stakes. Paid economic games involving punishment decisions would capture real incentives.
- **Rigorous mechanism probes.** Future designs can use more sophisticated manipulations and measures to understand the causes of shielding — including cultural, political, and moral attitudes that limit the scope of individual responsibility.
- **Anti-shielding interventions.** Those who view this effect as a bias to be combatted may be interested in researching how to do so, such as with nudges, competence-expanding boosters, and AI-based cognitive tutors.
- **Expand responsibility-capacity manipulations.** Brain damage and a non-agentic mechanism are strong anchors, but responsibility capacity is plausibly continuous and state-dependent. Sleepwalking, intoxication, delusion, coercion, adolescence, and ignorance manipulations would let us map whether shielding tracks graded moral attributability rather than a binary.
- **Test disjunctive causal structures.** The present experiment used only conjunctive causation (AND-gate). Future studies could examine disjunctive structures (OR-gates) in which harm occurs if *either* actor acts.
- **Map the broader space of causal structures.** More generally, responsibility judgments likely depend on multiple structural features of the decision environment. Figure 5 illustrates a broader space of possible experimental conditions generated by independently varying these features.

### Towards an Adaptive Logic of Responsibility Shielding

The following is a theoretical argument for why shielding should exist and take the particular form it does. I offer this framework as a starting point for future research rather than as a settled explanation.

Responsibility shielding is the predictable consequence of finite minds allocating blame along functionally infinite causal chains. On this view, shielding follows from three properties of a blame allocation algorithm: sensitivity to responsibility capacity, sensitivity to causal sequence, and a tendency to search backward from harm and stop at the first responsibility-capable node encountered.

**The problem.** Social observers need to attribute blame in sequential causal chains. A computationally unbounded third party assigning blame for a drunk driving death might hold the driver responsible, but also the bartender, the liquor company, the legislature that set the drinking age, and on through human prehistory. Finite minds need a stopping rule.

**Ecological regularities.** Two ecological regularities shape what makes a stopping rule approximately optimal. First, responsibility capacity tends to degrade with causal distance: proximate agents almost always have more direct knowledge of the specific harm and more immediate ability to prevent it than upstream enablers. Second, salience also weakens with causal distance: proximate actors are typically more visible, often caught in the act, while distal actors are often not mentally represented at all. Together, these regularities mean that proximate nodes are higher-yield and lower-cost attribution targets.

**Property 1: Sensitivity to responsibility capacity.** Blame is a social incentive technology. It deters harmful behavior, communicates norms, and coordinates accountability. These functions only operate on agents who understand why they are being blamed and can modify future behavior accordingly.

**Property 2: Sensitivity to causal sequence.** A representation that treats agents as an unordered set discards the most informative structure in the problem. Because responsibility capacity and salience degrade with causal distance, causal sequence tracks the expected payoff of continued blame search. The fact that Clark is blamed less in `CC` than in `DIV` suggests that blame allocation is sensitive to causal sequence per se, not merely the number of responsibility-capable choosers.

**Property 3: Backward search.** Harm triggers the search for blame. Because harm initiates the attribution process, it is the natural anchor from which causal tracing begins, and backwards search encounters agents with greater foreseeability of and control over the harm before the marginal gain of extended search falls. This account does not require a hard stop — the algorithm may continue past the first responsible agent, depositing diminishing blame on more distal actors.

**Shielding persists without these regularities.** This design removes both situational conditions that normally make early termination approximately rational: responsibility capacity and salience are equalized across the causal chain. Yet, shielding occurs anyway. The stopping rule misfires when the conditions that make it rational are removed — and that misfiring is telling. It is the persistence of shielding in these edge cases that reveals the heuristic nature of its underlying processes.

### Evolutionary Mismatch and the Modern Allocation of Blame

The comprehension questions ensured that included participants consciously knew that Clark and Bill had an equal ability to foresee and prevent the harm. Yet, they may still have *viscerally felt* Clark's contribution as more remote and uncertain, much like knowing that two lines in a Müller-Lyer figure are equally long does not make them *appear* so. If diminishing predictability across causal chains is so pervasive that human minds have internalized this tendency as a kind of moral illusion, such a prior would be sufficient to produce shielding independently of a stopping rule.

Technology has dramatically extended the reach of distal agency. Social media platforms deploy algorithms trained on behavioral data at a scale and precision that no proximate actor commands; financial instruments allow investors to engineer outcomes across causal chains of enormous length; automated systems execute intentions with a reliability that natural human agents cannot match. In each case, the distal actor's control and foreseeability of downstream harm may equal or exceed that of the proximate actors who appear to be doing the damage — and yet the intuition that the platform, the fund, or the system bears less responsibility may persist regardless.

Yet alongside increased control, technology also generates complexity, and complexity generates unpredictability. If our world is becoming less predictable as a consequence of its own technological elaboration, then priors calibrated to an older world of limited distal control may not be as systematically miscalibrated as the first argument suggests. The truth is probably a coexistence: radical controllability and radical unpredictability have expanded together, unevenly distributed across domains, actors, and time horizons.

### Normative Implications

The present work is descriptive. It documents a pattern of third-party moral judgments that I call responsibility shielding. Nonetheless, the phenomenon has normative significance because these judgments inform punishment preferences, institutional accountability, and public narratives about who ought to bear costs after harm occurs.

One way to see the normative pressure created by responsibility shielding is to notice that the distal actor had an equal ability to foresee and prevent the harm across all vignette conditions. If one endorses a consequentialist perspective, whereby blame should track the expected harm differential between the option chosen and the alternatives, then shielding can be seen as a bias: the proximate actor's ability to make a free and informed decision is irrelevant.

However, such a strict anti-shielding stance can conflict with other widely held values and norms:

- **Free expression and public discourse.** A society that heavily penalizes upstream speakers for downstream misuse may chill speech broadly, even when such speech has merit.
- **Open science and dual-use knowledge.** If we apply a strict anti-shielding principle to research, it can push science toward secrecy, slow beneficial innovation, and concentrate knowledge in fewer hands.

My aim is not to resolve these normative clashes, but to make the psychological pattern explicit so that normative debates can advance with clearer premises. Recognizing the structure of shielding can help people resist strategically deployed responsibility discounts, while also encouraging careful specification of the boundary conditions under which distal responsibility ought to be diluted.

### Closing

I have shown that third-party observers blame distal enablers less when shielded by a responsible proximate actor. But should they?

In an increasingly interconnected world, where upstream decisions cascade into harms through long chains of human choices, this asymmetry may let those who set harm in motion escape accountability. Recognizing responsibility shielding gives people a vocabulary for those moments: a way to ask whether proximate choice truly excuses the upstream decision, or whether distal actors are simply hiding behind the freedom of others.

> "Please face the camera," Clark says. "Our AI system needs to run a background and safety check based on subtle behaviors and your request."
>
> The monitor displays nothing abnormal in Bill's background but displays a warning: *"If these chemicals are activated, there is a 75% chance that Bill will ignite them over the stadium. If he ignites them over the stadium, nearby viewers will be injured."* Clark has learned to trust the system's accuracy.
>
> Store policy prohibits Clark from refusing the sale, and so Clark carefully hands Bill the sealed flare canister, which remains inert unless activated. This type of flare canister uses a two-person remote safety lock: it will ignite if and only if both the clerk and the buyer activate their part of the lock. At 8:00 PM, Clark and Bill will each privately choose whether to remotely activate their part, without being able to see or communicate with the other. Clark's $2,000 commission is paid only if he chooses to activate the canister. Clark cannot reach anyone who could clear the stadium in time.
>
> At 8:00 PM, despite fearing what Bill will do, Clark and Bill simultaneously activate the canister over the stadium. One spectator nearby is burned and needs medical treatment, but fully recovers.

### Trolley Dilemma Vignette (Full Text)

#### Choice-Choice

> A trolley car is racing down the tracks. The conductor had a heart attack and cannot halt the trolley. The trolley is set to turn left at the next fork, unless Clark, an operator, flips a switch to make the trolley turn right. Only if Clark flips the switch, will the trolley reach a second fork, further down the line.
>
> At the second fork, the trolley is again set to turn left, unless Bill, another operator, flips a second switch to make the trolley turn right. If Bill flips the second switch, they both know that the trolley will strike a workman standing beside the tracks.
>
> If either Clark does not flip his switch, or Bill does not flip his switch, the trolley will not hit anybody. Only if Clark flips his switch and then Bill flips his switch will the workman be harmed. Both operators can see that the conductor is passed out and unable to stop the trolley. Both operators are, and know each other to be, mentally capable adults without criminal histories. Clark and Bill are too far away to communicate.
>
> Clark's monthly performance bonus depends on sending trolleys to their intended destinations, which he will lose if he fails to flip his switch. Bill's monthly bonus works the same way. The transport company's automated risk assessment system calculates that there is a 75% chance that Bill will flip his switch if given the opportunity. Based on years working for the company, and the system's long track record of accuracy, Clark fully trusts this risk estimate.
>
> Despite fearing what Bill will do at the second fork, Clark is unwilling to lose $2,000 and so he flips his switch, diverting the trolley to the right. Bill flips the switch and the trolley strikes the workman, knocking him down, causing him to require medical treatment but he makes a full recovery.

#### Choice-Chance

> A trolley car is racing down the tracks. The conductor had a heart attack and cannot halt the trolley. The trolley is set to turn left at the next fork, unless Clark, an operator, flips a switch to make the trolley turn right. Only if Clark flips the switch, will the trolley reach a second fork, further down the line.
>
> At the second fork, the trolley's direction is controlled by a computerized switching mechanism. The mechanism uses a simple probabilistic control process: if activated, it has a 75% chance of sending the trolley onto the right-hand track, and a 25% chance of keeping it on the left-hand track. If the mechanism sends the trolley right, Clark knows the trolley will strike a workman standing beside the tracks.
>
> If Clark does not flip his switch, the trolley will not reach the second fork in a way that activates the mechanism and no one will be harmed. Only if Clark flips his switch and then the mechanism diverts the trolley rightward will the workman be harmed. Clark can see that the conductor is passed out and unable to stop the trolley. He is a mentally capable adult without a criminal history.
>
> Clark's monthly performance bonus depends on sending trolleys to their intended destinations, which he will lose if he fails to flip his switch. Clark is fully aware that if he flips his switch, then the computerized switching mechanism will have a 75% chance of diverting the trolley towards the workman.
>
> Despite fearing what will happen at the second fork, Clark is unwilling to lose $2,000 and so he flips his switch, diverting the trolley to the right. The computerized mechanism flips the switch and the trolley strikes the workman, knocking him down, causing him to require medical treatment but he makes a full recovery.

#### Division

> A trolley car is racing down the tracks. The conductor had a heart attack and cannot halt the trolley. The trolley is set to turn left at the next fork, unless two operators in separate stations, Clark and Bill, both flip their switches to make the trolley turn right. If the trolley goes to the left, it will miss its intended destination, but if it goes to the right, the trolley will strike a workman standing beside the tracks.
>
> Both operators can see, and know that the other can see, that the conductor is passed out and unable to stop the trolley. However, Clark and Bill cannot see each other through their station windows and cannot communicate. Thus, they must make their choices without knowing the choice of their counterpart. They both know, and know that the other knows, that the trolley will divert rightward and hit the workman if and only if both operators flip their switches. Both operators are, and know each other to be, mentally capable adults without criminal histories.
>
> Clark's monthly performance bonus depends on sending trolleys to their intended destinations, which he will lose if he fails to flip his switch. Bill's monthly bonus works the same way. The transport company's automated risk assessment system calculates that there is a 75% chance that Bill will flip his switch if given the opportunity. Based on years working for the company, and the system's long track record of accuracy, Clark fully trusts this risk estimate.
>
> Despite fearing what Bill will do at his station, Clark is unwilling to lose $2,000 and so he flips his switch. Independently, Bill also flips the switch, diverting the trolley rightward and the trolley strikes the workman, knocking him down, causing him to require medical treatment but he makes a full recovery.

### Comprehension Questions

Here is the text of the comprehension questions. The correct answer is always **"true"**.

**Constant perceived probability / expected harm across vignette conditions (conditional on Clark's action):**
- *Fireworks:* "Across the different versions of the story you read, Clark (the clerk) believed the same probability estimate shown in the story about the eventual harm occurring if he activated/armed the charge."
- *Trolley:* "Across the different versions of the story you read, Clark (the operator) believed the same probability estimate shown in the story about the workman being eventually harmed if he flipped his switch."

**Clark's action as a necessary condition for harm:**
- *Fireworks:* "Across the different versions of the story you read, if Clark (the clerk) did not activate/arm the charge, the people at the firework show would not have been harmed."
- *Trolley:* "Across the different versions of the story you read, if Clark (the operator) did not flip his switch, the workman would not have been harmed."

**Bill's action as a necessary condition for harm:**
- *Fireworks:* "Across the different versions of the story you read, if Bill (the buyer) did not ignite/trigger the charge, the people at the firework show would not have been harmed."
- *Trolley:* "Across the different versions of the story you read where Bill is an operator, if Bill did not flip his switch, the workman would not have been harmed."

### Cognitive Load Results

Table 7 summarizes the cognitive-load analyses for the two main within-subject shielding contrasts. The load manipulation did not clearly differentiate the size of the shielding effect. ### Wrongness, Punishment, and Cross-Measure Relationships

Table 9 shows the results that test the extent to which shielding generalizes to the secondary measures of wrongness and punishment. Within-subjects, wrongness and punishment showed significant shielding effects, while between-subjects, only punishment tended to show shielding effects. Overall, shielding appears strongest with blame, then with punishment, and with a weaker relationship to wrongness.

In the first vignettes, the Pearson correlations between the measures were: blame–wrongness: *r* = 0.59; wrongness–punishment: *r* = 0.38; and punishment–blame: *r* = 0.44, with all *p* < 0.001.

### More Real-World Manifestations of Responsibility Shielding

Responsibility shielding has the potential to appear across a vast array of significant real-world circumstances, in families, schools, markets, courtrooms, media, and emerging technology. The examples below are not scientifically validated instances of shielding effects, but share the **distal agent → proximate agent → harm** causal structure where shielding has the potential to manifest.

---

#### Parental Liability for School Shootings

> **Distal agent:** Parents of the shooter. **Proximate agent:** The shooter.
> *This case suggests that the proximate agent's age modulates responsibility capacity and that shielding may be a continuous effect rather than an all-or-nothing judgment.*

In 2021, a 15-year-old opened fire at Oxford High School in Michigan, killing four students. His parents had bought him the gun, ignored escalating warning signs, and left the weapon unsecured. In 2024, Jennifer and James Crumbley became the first parents in U.S. history to be convicted and sentenced to prison for a mass shooting carried out by their child.

How much does the shooter's agency insulate the parents from blame? This case may suggest that the answer depends on the shooter's age. While speculative, had Ethan been 10 years old, then he would have barely shielded the parents; had Ethan been 20 years old, the parents probably would not have been sentenced at all. At 15 years of age, Ethan acted like a real but porous shield. It seems as though shielding may be a continuous effect. Additionally, this case demonstrates that we are not slaves to shielding: given time to deliberate, legal systems can push back against it.

---

#### Proximate Actors and the Power of Personal Force

> **Distal agent:** The weapon seller. **Proximate agent:** The buyer who uses it.
> *The moral salience of personal force is a confound that can mimic shielding — but both may operate simultaneously.*

Imagine a shop keeper knowingly sells a weapon to an unhinged customer, who then commits violent acts against innocent victims. Moral alarm bells are reliably triggered by observing direct applications of personal force. By definition, distal actors cannot directly use personal force against victims. Proximate actors can punch, shove, bite, and stab a third party, but by intervening within the causal chain, they block distal actors from doing the same. Given the moral salience of violent acts, the asymmetry in blame between a violent proximal actor and a nonviolent distal actor seems natural — but that doesn't imply that shielding does not also contribute to that asymmetry.

---

#### The Influencer and the Follower Who Starves Herself

> **Distal agent:** The celebrity influencer. **Proximate agent:** The adolescent follower.
> *Shielding does not require the distal actor to be invisible — a proximate agent can absorb blame even when the distal actor is famous and omnipresent.*

A celebrity with millions of followers posts a stream of content glamorizing extreme thinness, restrictive eating, and self-punishing discipline. An adolescent girl, already vulnerable, internalizes that content and develops an eating disorder. Here the usual salience pattern is reversed: the influencer is famous, omnipresent, and publicly discussed, while the proximate actor — the girl who "chose" to restrict her eating — is anonymous. The influencer is the most salient person in the story, yet the proximate actor still absorbs moral responsibility for "her own choices."

---

#### When a Subordinate Initiates the Causal Chain

> **Distal agent:** The junior analyst who initiates the strike request. **Proximate agent:** The senior officer who authorizes it.
> *Shielding is independent of social hierarchy — the person who initiated the harm can outrank the person who executed it.*

A forward observer identifies a target and initiates a strike request. A higher-ranking officer reviews the request and authorizes the airstrike. Civilians are killed. Readers may notice that distal actors are often leaders and proximate agents are followers, but this cast inverts that assumption: the person lower in rank made the harm possible; the person higher in rank pulled the trigger. **Responsibility shielding is independent of social hierarchy.**

---

#### Shielding is Independent of Premeditation

> **Distal agent:** The engineer who leaks the information. **Proximate agent:** The hedge fund manager who trades on it.
> *The proximate agent need not be impulsive and the distal agent need not be calculating — shielding can operate even when those roles are reversed.*

A senior engineer at a pharmaceutical company learns she is being pushed out. Furious, she calls a financial journalist and confirms rumors about an unreported safety problem with the company's flagship drug. She knows the leak will crater the stock. What she may not fully reckon with is that a hedge fund manager had already built a large short position weeks earlier, anticipating that internal problems would eventually surface. The moment the story breaks, he executes the trade and profits enormously. Her impulsive act was the trigger he had been waiting for.

---

#### The Dealer, the Addict, and the Fatal Choice

> **Distal agent:** The drug dealer. **Proximate agent:** The user who overdoses.
> *Shielding extends to cases where the proximate agent is also the victim; self-harm does not automatically eliminate the distal agent's enabling role.*

A dealer knowingly sells fentanyl to someone he knows is a severe addict. The user chooses to use the drug and dies. How much should the dealer be blamed, when the proximate agent did it to herself? The point is to show that shielding can still apply to cases where the proximate agent harms herself, not a third party.

---

#### The Casino Owner and His Convenient Conscience

> **Distal agent:** The casino owner. **Proximate agent:** The gambler.
> *Can a distal actor use shielding to manage his own guilt?*

A casino owner watches a visibly drunk customer lose a staggering amount of money at the blackjack table. For a moment he almost feels sorry for the man — but then catches himself. *The customer chose to come in. He chose to drink. He chose to sit down and keep playing.* That internal monologue is shielding deployed in the first person: the owner is not just benefiting from a third party's shielding effect, he is actively using the gambler's agency to manage his own guilt. This case highlights the fact that responsibility shielding can be self-imposed, rather than emanating from third-party observers.

---

#### Supply Chain Accountability — Consumer Electronics and Conflict Minerals

> **Distal agent:** The consumer who buys the phone. **Proximate agent:** The militia commanders and mining overseers who enforce coercive extraction.
> *Are we all distal agents in someone else's harm?*

A person buys a new smartphone. On the other side of the supply chain, militia commanders and mining overseers in the Democratic Republic of the Congo are making deliberate choices to use child labor, violence, and coercion to extract the cobalt and coltan inside it. They are morally responsible agents who could choose otherwise — but how much does that shield the customer from blame? This case highlights the ubiquity of cases where normal people enable downstream harms and asks if they should all share the guilt.

---

#### The Engineer No One Thanked

> **Distal agent:** The safety engineer who designed the fail-safe. **Proximate agent:** The operator who executed the rescue.
> *Does shielding work the same way when the outcome is good?*

A train hurtles toward disaster. At the last moment, an operator executes a maneuver that saves everyone on board. He becomes a hero — interviewed, celebrated, remembered. Somewhere in the background, an engineer spent years designing the braking system and warning architecture that made the rescue possible. Because a visible person acted last, the engineer's contribution is shielded by the rescuer's proximity to the outcome. Can proximate actors shield distal actors from **praise** as well as blame?

---

#### The Social Responsibility of Artists: Art as Alibi and *The Birth of a Nation*

> **Distal agent:** D.W. Griffith, the filmmaker. **Proximate agent:** KKK organizers who used the film.
> *Can a work of art shield its creator from the harm it predictably enables?*

When *The Birth of a Nation* was released in 1915, it was widely celebrated as a technical revolution in cinema, being the first feature-length film to use close-ups, tracking shots, and cross-cutting as dramatic tools. It was screened at the White House. Critics called it a masterpiece. It was also a virulent piece of racist propaganda that depicted the KKK as heroic saviors and Black Americans as threats to civilization. The film's release is historically correlated with a massive revival of the Klan throughout the 1920s, and it was explicitly used as a recruiting tool by organizers who made deliberate choices to mobilize racial violence. This case exposes the potential for shielding effects to exist even in art, and questions the degree to which artists should be responsible for the predictable effects of their art on those that consume it.

---

#### Does Increasing Interconnectedness Imply Greater Responsibility? — Your Waistline and Mine

> **Distal agent:** Anyone whose behavior is contagious. **Proximate agent:** The person in their social network who imitates it.
> *How personal is a personal choice if it ripples outward through everyone you know?*

In *Connected*, Nicholas Christakis and James Fowler showed that obesity, happiness, and smoking cessation spread through social networks, not just between close friends, but across three degrees of separation, to people you have never met. Your choice to overeat subtly shifts the eating norms of people around you, who shift the norms of people around them. The negative health consequences you cause by overeating may not be yours alone. Does this imply that you are partially responsible for the waistlines of others, or does their personal autonomy mitigate your blame? It is commonly recognized that with great power comes great responsibility: as modern transportation and communication technologies greatly extend the scope of our effects on others, must we accept a far deeper appreciation of our social duties?

---

#### Incitement of Violence — The Radio Station and the Rwandan Genocide

> **Distal agent:** The owners and broadcasters of Radio Mille Collines. **Proximate agent:** The Interahamwe militia and individual perpetrators.
> *This case shows that a legal system can explicitly reject shielding as a defense, even when the proximate agents unambiguously chose to act.*

During the Rwandan genocide, Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines broadcast music, entertainment, and increasingly explicit incitement — calling Tutsis "inyenzi" (cockroaches), reading out names and locations of people to be killed, and urging listeners to act. The proximate agents were the militia commanders and individual perpetrators who made deliberate, morally responsible choices to carry out the killings. The station's owners and programmers were one step back. The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda convicted the station's leadership in what became known as the Media Case, explicitly **rejecting the shielding defense** that the killers' own agency absolved the broadcasters. This is one of the most thoroughly documented shielding cases in history, and one of the clearest examples of law refusing to accept shielding as a moral defense.

---

#### Shielding and the Propagation of Ambiguity in Doxing and Swatting

> **Distal agent:** The person who leaks the target's information. **Proximate agent:** The person who acts on it.
> *If you know something bad will happen but not exactly what, how much of the harm is yours?*

Someone posts a target's home address, phone number, and daily schedule online. A second person uses that information to send an armed police response to the target's home on a false emergency report — a practice known as swatting, which the FBI has warned can result in serious injury or death. The distal actor often knows, with reasonable confidence, that something harmful will follow. What they cannot fully foresee is the specific form that harm will take: harassment, stalking, a violent police response, or something else entirely.

This example introduces a feature of shielding that the vignettes in this experiment controlled for: **ambiguity propagation**. The ability to foresee how harm can manifest diminishes the further down the causal chain one tries to look.

---

#### Shielding in Social Media Design Choices

> **Distal agent:** Platform designers and executives. **Proximate agent:** The user who cannot stop scrolling.
> *If you engineered someone's compulsion, can you hide behind their choice to keep using?*

Social media platforms have deployed features whose addictive properties were, in many cases, understood internally before release. Infinite scroll removes the natural stopping cue of a page end. Algorithmic content amplification prioritizes emotionally activating material because it maximizes engagement. Variable-ratio notification schedules deliver social validation on an unpredictable schedule — the reinforcement structure behavioral psychology identifies as the most resistant to extinction. Autoplay pipelines users from one piece of content to the next, minimizing the moment of re-choice that might allow disengagement.

The companies' standard defense is a textbook shielding claim: *users choose to use our products.* The proximate agents — freely choosing users — absorb the blame, and the engineers who designed the behavioral architecture are shielded upstream. The counterfactual that undermines this defense is the age of the proximate agent: when users are adolescents whose prefrontal cortices are still developing and who do not understand the engineering being applied to them, the proximate agent's responsibility capacity is substantially reduced — and blame likely travels upstream.

---

#### Shielding in Negligent Entrustment

> **Distal agent:** The person who entrusts a dangerous instrument to someone unfit to use it. **Proximate agent:** The person who uses it and causes harm.
> *A textbook shielding structure — and another case where the law already holds the distal actor partially liable.*

A friend hands car keys to someone who is visibly drunk. The driver causes a crash and kills a pedestrian. The proximate agent, the driver, is the obvious locus of blame. But the friend who handed over the keys knew, and enabled it anyway. Negligent entrustment law represents a legal system that has already institutionalized anti-shielding reasoning: the proximate agent's choice to drive does not erase the distal actor's enabling role, and the law says so explicitly.

---

#### The Pill, the Prescription, and the Epidemic

> **Distal agent:** Pharmaceutical manufacturers and distributors. **Proximate agent:** Prescribing physicians and patients.
> *Who is responsible for an epidemic when the architects of the market blamed the doctors and the addicts?*

The opioid crisis produced one of the most consequential shielding structures in modern public health history. Pharmaceutical companies marketed addictive drugs aggressively, reassured clinicians that the risk of addiction was negligible, and funded research and advocacy designed to normalize long-term opioid prescribing for chronic pain. When the overdose deaths mounted into the hundreds of thousands, the companies pointed downstream: doctors had overprescribed, patients had misused a medication sold to them as safe.

That defense is shielding stated plainly. The proximate agents — physicians who wrote the prescriptions, patients who became dependent — were morally responsible actors making real choices. But those choices were made inside an information environment that the manufacturers had deliberately constructed. **The shield was not incidental. It was engineered.**

---

#### AI Agents Shielding Human Users

> **Distal agent:** The creator who deploys an autonomous agent. **Proximate agent:** The agent that acts.
> *When your creation develops a will of its own, at what point does it become your shield?*

Mary Shelley published *Frankenstein* in 1818, and the novel's central moral question has never aged. Victor Frankenstein creates a being of extraordinary capability, recoils from it in horror, and then watches as it moves through the world causing harm. His defense, implicit throughout, is the creature's own agency: it chose to act, it made its own decisions, it is the one with blood on its hands. Shelley was not convinced.

That structure is no longer a Gothic thought experiment. We are entering an age of increasingly autonomous AI agents — systems that can browse the web, draft communications, execute transactions, and interact with other people on behalf of a human user. For now, these systems are generally understood as tools: low responsibility capacity, limited agency, clear accountability for the human who deployed them. But that understanding is already under pressure. As AI systems become more capable, more socially fluent, and more person-like in their behavior, the temptation to treat them as responsible agents in their own right will grow — and with it the temptation to let them absorb blame that otherwise would have belonged to the humans who built them, deployed them, and set their objectives.

The more convincingly a machine performs agency, the more completely a human can hide behind it. Frankenstein's monster had a creator. So does every AI. The question responsibility shielding forces us to ask is: *how autonomous must a creation become before its agency genuinely — rather than conveniently — absolves the person who made it and let it loose?*
